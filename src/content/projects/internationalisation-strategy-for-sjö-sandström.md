# Summary

Sjöö Sandström should pursue active internationalisation, but through a different methodology than used in earlier expansion. Previous country-driven entry produced a scattered footprint of weak relationships across distant markets that generate neither volume nor brand-building leverage. Partner-driven entry, concentrated in fewer well-chosen markets, is the alternative.

Three markets are recommended for active investment, and the company’s existing scattered presence elsewhere should be wound down to e-commerce-only servicing. Poland is the highest-priority entry: EU single-market access removes friction, Western-aligned tastes that match the brand, strong economic growth supporting rising HNWI numbers, and the absence of established independent watchmakers leaves genuine room for an accessible-independent-luxury position. The United States should be maintained at light commitment, with the existing position supported through direct e-commerce, enthusiast-media coverage in publications such as Hodinkee and Worn & Wound, and selective retail without significant new capital deployment. Japan, the strongest cultural fit but the most demanding entry, follows once production capacity and partner relationships justify the move. 

Three further recommendations support this strategy. First, exit scattered legacy markets around the world, wind these down to e-commerce-only to serve existing customers, and redirect management attention to target markets. Second, treat partner selection as the strategic decision at every level of commitment, not country selection alone. Third, leverage two distinctive but underused assets in marketing. The institutional client portfolio (Swedish Air Force, Coast Guard, Special police units), and the early position in esports sponsorship, to reach craft-credible audiences without competing on advertising spend.

Production capacity remains a hard ceiling on simultaneous growth across markets. The staged sequence therefore respects this constraint and requires disciplined adherence to milestones rather than opportunistic acceleration.

# 1. Introduction

This report evaluates Sjöö Sandström’s internationalisation strategy and recommends a path forward. Previous international expansion produced a scattered footprint of single-retailer relationships across distant markets, generating mixed outcomes. The strategic question is therefore one of methodology rather than principle.

The company’s strengths lie in craft credibility, design heritage, and institutional client relationships. Its weaknesses are scale, marketing budget, and a fragmented international presence. The opportunity is to concentrate effort where craft positioning commands a premium and where partner-led entry is feasible.

The report addresses internationalisation rationale, market selection, entry strategy and market environment, marketing mix, and recommendations.

# 2. The Case for internationalisation

For Sjöö Sandström, internationalisation is not a strategic choice but a structural necessity dictated by the nature of the product, the buyer, and the category itself. The company is already present in international markets, but the question is whether to deepen and refocus that presence or retreat. This report argues for a deepening on different principles than what guided earlier expansion, and explains why de-internationalisation would amount to strategic self-harm.

Three arguments support this position. First, the Swedish domestic market is structurally too small to sustain an exclusive watchmaker. Although the 25 000-50 000 SEK price range is positioned to attract a broad customer base, buyers of hand-finished, individually-numbered timepieces are in the low hundreds annually within Sweden. A small-scale manufacturer can produce at this volume, but cannot sustainably grow, invest in craft, or build brand equity at this scale. The Swedish market is right now a launchpad, not a preferred market.

Second, the luxury watch buyer is inherently global. While the watch customer base spans a wide spectrum, from casual buyers to dedicated collectors, the more engaged segment inhabits a largely transnational network. Many of these collectors read the same international publications, follow the same auction houses, attend the same fairs, and discuss the same brands across forums and social media. A watchmaker’s visibility within this network is therefore central to its survival as an independent. The category itself is borderless. Even historical archetypes of the modern watches, such as pilot watches, divers’ watches, and GMT watches, were designed for people traveling around the world. internationalisation is encoded within the history of the product category.

Third, Swedish heritage is a more competitive asset when sold abroad. Within Sweden, “Swedish craftsmanship” is more unremarkable compared to in Tokyo, Munich, or Warsaw, where it is the differentiator that justifies a premium against Swiss benchmarks. Rolex provides the instructive parallel, founded in London and only later relocated to Geneva, the brand built global equity by transcending its national origin while drawing strength from it. Sjöö Sandström must follow the same pattern, Swedish heritage is again a launchpad, not a ceiling.

Theoretically, this recommendation aligns with the Uppsala model (Johanson and Vahlne, 2009), which reframes internationalisation as a process of building network position rather than gradually crossing psychic distance. For a luxury watchmaker, the relevant network is the global collector ecosystem, and commitment should deepen as the position within that ecosystem is established. Geographic proximity remains a useful secondary advantage where it reduces operational friction, but it is no longer the primary driver of market selection.

# 3. Market Selection

##   3.1 Screening Criteria

Following Hollensen’s (2020) sequential screening framework, candidate markets are filtered through three stages. Preliminary screening eliminates countries on broad macro grounds, political stability, GDP per capita, and currency risk. Industry-specific screening then evaluates luxury watch market size, growth, and retail infrastructure. Firm-specific screening, the decisive stage for Sjöö Sandström, applies criteria tied to the company’s particular profile: HNWI population and growth, luxury watch market size, acceptance of non-Swiss craft, ease of market entry, and demand for accessible-luxury watches.

Five candidate markets emerged from preliminary screening: the United States, Japan, Germany, Poland, and South Korea. Switzerland, China, and the UAE were eliminated. Switzerland, because it is the wrong battlefield for a Swedish challenger, China due to political and demand-side volatility, and the UAE because its brand-driven luxury culture undervalues the craft positioning.

## 3.2 Selected Markets

The matrix yields a clear ranking, but the recommended portfolio has been selected to prioritise strategic complementarities over aggregated score-tracking. The three selected markets, United States, Japan, and Poland, together cover three distinct positioning plays. The US has a deep enthusiast segment that if delivered right will provide a solid foundation and make it easier to capture market shares within the enthusiast segment. Japan has a strong cultural history of valuing handcrafted and well-designed items. Poland has great opportunities to gain market shares for an accessible luxury watch. Germany, despite scoring 20, is omitted because it offers no unique white space. A.Lange & Söhne and Nomos already occupy the strongest non-Swiss German watchmaking positions, and Germany’s main strategic asset, being EU access, is duplicated by Poland.

### United States

**The largest single destination market for Swiss luxury watch exports, accounting for 17% of Swiss watch exports in 2025 (FH, 2026), with 7,9 million HNWIs growing at 7,6% annually (Capgemini, 2025).** The relevant target segment is not the mainstream luxury-driven segment, but the enthusiast collector, readers of magazines like Hodinkee and Worn & Wound, members of RedBar groups, and buyers who prize independent makers. Brands such as Nomos and Christopher Ward have built sustainable US positions in exactly this segment. Tariff exposure under the current administration is real but manageable with the EU baseline rate of 15%, leaving Sjöö Sandström at a structural advantage relative to Swiss competitors facing higher rates.

### Japan

**The strongest cultural fit among the three.** Japanese collectors prize visible craft over status symbolism, and the domestic success of Grand Seiko demonstrates that non-Swiss heritage watchmaking is not only tolerated but actively celebrated. Scandinavian design carries an unusual appreciation within Japanese culture. Japan ranks second only to the US in Swiss watch imports (FH, 2026), but its strategic value lies less in raw size than in fit. Japan rewards exactly the qualities Sjöö Sandström produces.

### Poland

**The creative pick.** Poland has been the EU’s fastest-growing major economy of the past decade, with HNWI numbers rising sharply (Knight Frank, 2024). As a fellow EU member, Sweden faces zero import duties, eliminating the punitive import duty regimes that prevent accessible-luxury positioning in many emerging markets. Crucially, Polish buyers face the same closed Rolex and Patek waiting lists as Western European collectors but with fewer independent alternatives established in-market, leaving genuine room for an “accessible independent luxury” position.

## 3.3 Theoretical Anchoring 

Porter’s Diamond helps explain why these markets reward Sjöö Sandström. Sweden’s national diamond for watchmaking is structurally weak with limited domestic demand, no related industry cluster, and minimal firm rivalry. The company cannot compete on ecosystem advantages, only on craft and design heritage, which forces it to seek markets where those specific assets are valued at a premium. Porter’s Value Chain reinforces the point that Sjöö Sandström’s competitive advantage lies in the finishing and inspection operations, which are visible only to buyers who appreciate them.

# 4. Entry Strategy and Market Environment

##   4.1 Market Environment

The three markets differ on the dimensions that matter most for a small craft watchmaker. How much retail friction sits between Sjöö Sandström and the buyer, how the buyer expects to be sold to, and how local conditions shape pricing and brand positioning.

##   4.2 Entry Mode Recommendations

Sjöö Sandström’s history of international expansions suggests that growth depends less on choosing the right country than on choosing the right partner. The recommendations below apply Hollensen’s entry mode spectrum within the constraint that in each market, the question is not only the level of commitment, but which partner unlocks it. The corollary is equally important, markets where no meaningful partner position exists should be exited rather than maintained as scattered legacy presence.

### Poland - Direct export with light strategic anchoring

Lowest commitment, highest priority. The foundation is a Polish-language e-commerce site with PLN pricing, fulfilled from Stockholm. This is paired with three lightweight entry strategies: targeted seeding of Polish watch and lifestyle press, a single curated launch event in Warsaw to introduce the brand to invited press and collectors, and one trial retail partner that is ideally a specialty watch retailer with collector credibility, carried on consignment rather than a full distribution agreement. The combined cost is modest, but the brand gains a physical reference point in the market and a foundation moment to build on. If milestones are met, the next step is converting the trial retail relationship into a formal partnership and adding a second city.

### United States - Light-touch presence, organic growth

Maintain the existing US position via direct e-commerce and selective enthusiast retail without significant new commitment. The US enthusiast segment finds craft brands through media and watch fairs rather than retail expansion, so press placement and watch fair appearances produce more leverage than capital deployment. Major strategic moves in the US should wait until Polish operations are stabilised.

### Japan - Specialty retail partnership with eventual boutique potential

The most demanding entry. Japanese collectors expect to handle a craft watch before purchase, and the market is gatekept by a small number of specialty retailers with curatorial authority. The right partner is one of these, not a department store or a luxury chain. Once the network position is established, evidenced by appearances at Watches & Wonders Tokyo and coverage in the Japanese watch press, a single small boutique in Tokyo becomes justifiable.

Across all three markets, commitment escalates only as defined milestones are met. This staged approach respects a hard production constraint, as an overcommitted launch into multiple markets simultaneously risks recreating the supply chain problems that earlier scattered expansion produced.

# 5. Marketing Mix Strategy

The marketing mix should resolve the central tension in Sjöö Sandström’s positioning as an exclusive craft brand at an accessible price point. Standardisation reinforces craft identity, and adaptation responds to local pricing and channel realities. The recommendations below standardise where the product and message are concerned, and adapt where price and channel are concerned

### Product

**Standardise**. The product is the Swedish craft identity, and adapting it for individual markets dilutes what makes it distinct. The Royal Steel, Landsort, and UTC lines should be sold in their existing form across all three markets. The one legitimate form of variation is market-launch limited editions, a small Warsaw, Tokyo, or US-launch series tied to local cultural reference points. This continues the company’s existing Annual Edition logic and serves as a network-building event for collectors in each market.

### Price

**Adapt with discipline.** Pricing should reflect local purchasing power and competitive context rather than mechanical currency conversion. In Japan, premium pricing of 10-15% above the Swedish equivalent matches buyer expectations that imported craft watches command an import premium. In the United States, near-parity pricing in USD absorbs the 15% EU tariff rather than passing it through, leaving Sjöö Sandström competitively positioned against Tudor and Nomos. In Poland, pricing 5-10% below the Swedish equivalent in PLN supports the accessible-independent-luxury positioning without crossing into discount territory.

### Place

**Selective and channel-light.** Distribution should remain thin and curated. Hand-picked specialty retail partners are the primary channel in each market, supported by direct e-commerce as a complementary reach mechanism, particularly valuable for serving buyers in regions without formal retail presence, but not a substitute for the in-person handling that craft watch buyers expect. Mass distribution and grey-market exposure are actively avoided. The existing B2B corporate gift business, where collaborations with major Swedish companies use the watches as anniversary gifts, represents an underexploited international opportunity, since multinational Swedish firms have offices and customer base in all three target markets.

### Promotion

**Earned media and community over paid advertising.** Sjöö Sandström cannot outspend Rolex's big budgets on advertising, and should not try to. The right strategy combines watch-enthusiast media coverage, trade fair presence, and selective seeding of credible watch creators across YouTube, Instagram, and TikTok in each target market. Two distinctive assets deserve sharp use. First, the company’s institutional client portfolio, including official watches for the Swedish Air Force, ongoing relationship with the Swedish Coast Guard and special police units, represents craft-credibility through professional trust, a positioning the Bremont brand has used effectively in the British context. Second, the early position in esports sponsorship, already pioneered by the company’s Counter-Strike presence, can be extended into adjacent categories. These are unconventional but on-brand. They put Sjöö Sandström in front of young, design-literate audiences without the costs of mainstream luxury advertising.

# 6. Conclusion and Recommendations

Sjöö Sandström should pursue active internationalisation, but on different principles from those that guided earlier expansion. The right question is not which countries to enter but which partners can carry the brand within them. Country-driven expansion produced a footprint of single-retailer relationships across distant markets that generate neither volume nor brand-building leverage. Partner-driven expansion in fewer, better-chosen markets is the alternative.

### Three recommendations follow

**Concentrate on three markets: Poland, the United States, and Japan.** Poland represents the highest-priority entry by having EU access that removes friction, Western-aligned tastes matching the brand, an increased economic growth, and the absence of established independent watchmakers, leaving genuine white space. The United States should be maintained at light commitment, with new investment held back until Polish operations are stabilised. Japan, the strongest cultural fit but the most demanding entry, follows once production capacity and partner relationships justify the move.

**Exit scattered legacy markets.** The current international presence should be wound down to e-commerce-only servicing of existing customers. The management attention, marketing investment, and supply allocation released by this consolidation are better deployed in the three target markets.

**Treat the partner selection as the strategic decision.** Within Hollensen’s entry-mode spectrum, partner quality is the binding constraint at every commitment level. The recommended approach is to invest in finding the right specialty retailers, watch creators, and institutional collaborators rather than in volume of presence.

Three risks merit ongoing monitoring: production capacity, which remains a hard ceiling on growth across multiple markets simultaneously. Tariff conditions in the United States may shift further. The staged commitment ladder, which requires disciplined adherence to milestones rather than opportunistic acceleration.
