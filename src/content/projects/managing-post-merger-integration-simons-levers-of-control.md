## Abstract

Mergers and acquisitions consistently fail to deliver their intended value, with post-merger integration (PMI) identified as the most critical determinant of success. Despite the recognized importance of management control systems in steering integration, the application of comprehensive control frameworks to PMI remains limited. This study investigates how managers use Simons’ (1995) four levers of control, belief systems, boundary systems, diagnostic control systems, and interactive control systems, during PMI.

Adopting an interpretivist philosophy and an abductive approach, eight semi-structured interviews were conducted with integration managers, planners, and consultants across different organizations. Thematic analysis was applied to the data, structured around the four levers while remaining open to emergent themes.

The findings reveal that the four levers are not used equally. Belief systems and interactive control systems were the most actively and confidently engaged, while diagnostic control systems were consistently present but treated as routine infrastructure, and boundary systems were consistently the weakest and most difficult for participants to articulate. A substitution effect was identified: in the predominantly Nordic management context of this study, belief systems appeared to perform much of the constraining function formally attributed to boundary systems, a pattern interpreted through Hofstede’s cultural dimensions and situated in relation to Tessier and Otley’s (2012) critique and Kruis et al.’s (2016) empirical work on balance configurations.

The study extends the LOC framework beyond single-case applications and contributes two theoretical refinements: that levers can substitute for one another rather than only complementing each other, and that the balance between them is shaped by national cultural context.

## Introduction

Mergers and acquisitions represent one of the most significant strategies available to firms seeking growth, market expansion, and competitive repositioning in the contemporary business landscape. The scale of global M&A activity reflects this strategic importance, with transactions spanning industries and national borders as organizations pursue synergies that organic growth alone cannot deliver (Cartwright & Schoenberg, 2006).

Despite the strategic appeal and the substantial financial resources committed to these transactions, the outcome of most mergers and acquisitions falls short of expectations. Research consistently reports that between 50 and 80 percent of M&As fail to achieve the synergies and value creation objectives that justified the deal (Cartwright, Tytherleigh & Robertson, 2007; Marks, 2006; Kyei-Poku & Miller, 2013). Given the scale of resources invested and the disruptive impact on employees, customers, and markets, understanding why so many transactions fail to deliver their intended value carries both academic and practical significance.

The source of this failure typically does not reside in the strategic rationale or financial structure of the deal itself. Rather, it is the post-merger integration (PMI) phase, the period following deal closure during which two previously independent organizations must be combined into a functioning entity, that has been identified as the most critical determinant of M&A success or failure (Epstein, 2004). It is during integration that theoretical synergies must be translated into operational reality, and it is here that the majority of value is either created or destroyed (Epstein, 2004; Gomes et al., 2013). Haspeslagh and Jemison (1991) characterize integration as an interactive and gradual process in which individuals from two organizations learn to work together and cooperate in the transfer of strategic capabilities, a description that underscores both the human complexity and the extended timeframe involved.

Integration is therefore a demanding, fundamentally human process, and cultural alignment between the merging organizations is widely cited as one of its most difficult aspects, with culture clashes contributing significantly to M&A failure (Kyei-Poku & Miller, 2013). When integrations cross national borders, this challenge intensifies further, as managers must also navigate differences in national culture alongside organizational culture, a phenomenon described as ‘double-layered acculturation’ (Barkema et al., 1996), alongside divergent regulatory environments and geographic distance (Wang et al., 2020).

Given the complexity and high stakes of PMI, the mechanisms through which managers steer and coordinate the process become critically important. Management control systems (MCS) provide the formal and informal tools through which organizations communicate strategic direction, monitor progress, set boundaries, and encourage learning and adaptation (Jones, 1985; Nilsson, 2002). During integration, these systems take on heightened significance as managers seek to align two organizations with potentially different strategies, performance expectations, and operating norms. Despite this recognized importance, research examining

how managers actually design and deploy control systems during PMI remains relatively limited. A small number of studies have investigated MCS in PMI contexts, including early work by Jones (1985), Granlund’s (2003) examination of management accounting system integration, Beusch’s (2007) study of contradicting control ideologies in cross-border acquisitions, and Hoque and Chia’s (2012) application of Simons’ Levers of Control (LOC) framework to a multinational subsidiary following an overseas takeover. However, significant gaps persist, particularly regarding the application of comprehensive multidimensional control frameworks to the integration phase.

Simons’ (1995) LOC framework identifies four levers, belief systems, boundary systems, diagnostic systems, and interactive control systems, whose explicit focus on how managers use control systems to implement and renew strategy makes it well-aligned with the demands of PMI. While Hoque and Chia (2012) demonstrated the framework’s utility in a single post-takeover case, broader application across multiple integrations and managerial perspectives remains underexplored. This gap provides the foundation for the present study.

### Research Question

How do managers use Simons’ four levers of control during post-merger integration?

## Literature Review

### Post-Merger Integration: Context and Challenges

Post-merger integration refers to the phase beginning immediately after deal closure, the moment often referred to as closing, and continuing until the acquired organisation is incorporated into the acquiring firm’s operations. Haspeslagh and Jemison (1991, p.106) define integration as “an interactive and gradual process in which individuals from two organizations learn to work together and cooperate in the transfer of strategic capabilities.” This definition is significant because it frames integration not as a technical or administrative exercise but as a fundamentally human process, one that unfolds over time and depends on the willingness and ability of individuals across both organisations to collaborate. It is during this phase that the strategic rationale behind the acquisition must be translated into operational reality, and theoretical synergies must become actual value creation (Gomes et al., 2013).

The academic literature on PMI has consistently identified integration as the most critical determinant of whether an acquisition ultimately succeeds or fails. Epstein (2004) argues that even mergers with fundamentally sound strategic rationales fail when the integration process is poorly designed or inadequately executed. This argument shifts analytical attention away

from deal characteristics, such as price, strategic fit, or market conditions, and toward the managerial processes that follow the transaction. Gomes et al. (2013) reinforce this perspective by mapping critical success factors across the M&A process and demonstrating that factors related to integration, rather than pre-deal strategy or negotiation, carry the greatest weight in determining post-acquisition performance.

Research has identified several factors consistently associated with successful integration. Epstein (2004) highlights four in particular: integration speed, communication, cultural management, and leadership. Each involves difficult trade-offs. Integration speed requires careful balance: moving too slowly prolongs uncertainty for employees and customers and risks losing momentum before synergies are realized, while moving too quickly risks overwhelming the organization’s capacity to absorb change and resolve emerging problems (Epstein, 2004). The question of pace is therefore not simply operational but strategic, depending on contextual judgment rather than standardized prescriptions.

Communication is equally complex. Research consistently links insufficient or poorly managed communication to integration failure (Epstein, 2004; Gomes et al., 2013). Effective communication must be frequent, two-directional, and sustained, serving to reduce employee anxiety, signal strategic direction, build trust between organizational groups, and surface problems as they emerge.

Cultural management represents perhaps the most extensively discussed challenge in PMI literature. Culture clashes between merging organizations are among the most commonly cited causes of M&A failure (Kyei-Poku & Miller, 2013). The difficulty arises because organizational culture encompasses deeply embedded values, assumptions, and behavioral norms that are taken for granted by those within the organization but become visible and contentious when two distinct cultures are brought together. When acquisitions cross national borders, this challenge intensifies. Barkema, Bell and Pennings (1996) describe this as a process of ‘double-layered acculturation,’ in which managers must simultaneously navigate differences in both national and organizational culture. National cultural differences influence leadership expectations, attitudes toward hierarchy and authority, communication styles, and tolerance for ambiguity, all of which directly affect how integration is experienced (Chen et al., 2022). Geographic distance compounds these difficulties by increasing information asymmetry and reducing the opportunities for informal, face-to-face interaction that often underpins trust-building between organizational groups (Wang et al., 2020).

Leadership during PMI operates as both a success factor in its own right and as the mechanism through which the others are managed, requiring deliberate managerial action to set direction, resolve conflicts, and sustain commitment through disruption, ideally beginning before deal closure with defined milestones (Epstein, 2004; Gomes et al., 2013).

A notable feature of the PMI literature is that it has devoted considerable attention to identifying what determines success and failure, but comparatively less to the practical mechanisms through which managers actually steer the process. What remains less well understood is how managers translate strategic integration objectives into day-to-day action,

how they monitor whether integration is progressing as intended, how they set boundaries for acceptable behavior during periods of upheaval, and how they create space for learning when plans encounter reality. These are fundamentally questions about management control, pointing toward the need for analytical frameworks that can capture the full range of tools available to managers during integration.

### Management Control Systems in Organisational Change

Management control systems provide the formal and informal mechanisms through which managers translate strategic intentions into coordinated organizational action. Anthony (1965, p.17) originally defined management control as “the process by which managers assure that resources are obtained and used effectively in the accomplishment of the organization’s objectives,” a definition later revised to emphasize strategy implementation: “the process by which managers influence other members of the organization to implement the organization’s strategies” (Anthony & Govindarajan, 2007, p.7). Contemporary scholars adopt broader conceptualizations, with Malmi and Brown (2008) and Ferreira and Otley (2009) including performance evaluation, reward systems, financial and non-financial information, and both formal and informal mechanisms. Simons (1994, p.170) likewise defines MCS as “the formal, information-based routines and procedures used by managers to maintain or alter patterns in organizational activities,” emphasizing that control systems serve not only to enforce compliance but also to enable innovation, experimentation, and the emergence of new strategic directions.

MCS are particularly relevant during post-merger integration because M&As create changes in strategy, organizational structure, power dynamics, and competitive environment (Hoque & Chia, 2012; Jones, 1985). During the uncertainty that characterizes the PMI phase, MCS can be reconsidered and adapted to manage integration. Simons’ (1994) longitudinal study of newly appointed top managers found that control system changes were among the most prevalent categories of organizational change during leadership transitions, with modifications occurring within the first three to six months in nearly every case studied. While that research focused on new managers rather than post-merger contexts, the underlying dynamic is closely analogous: managers entering situations of strategic change use control systems to overcome inertia, communicate new agendas, establish timetables and targets, and sustain attention to new initiatives (Simons, 1994). MCS thereby assist in undertaking post-takeover changes, maintaining routines, ensuring efficiency, and encouraging desired behavior (Jones, 1985; Merchant & Van der Stede, 2017), while coordinating and communicating strategy, vision, and goals to create shared understanding and commitment within combined business units (Nilsson, 2002; Jordão et al., 2014).

Several studies have examined MCS specifically within PMI contexts. Jones (1985) provided early evidence that management accounting systems play an active role following acquisitions, serving as mechanisms through which acquiring firms assert control over target

companies. Nilsson (2002) extended this by showing that control systems served to articulate new visions and create shared commitment within combined business units. Granlund (2003) offered a more cautionary perspective through a case study of management accounting system integration, revealing that technical challenges, particularly incompatible information systems and KPIs between merging firms, created significant barriers; existing MCS often lacked the sophistication to handle planned post-M&A growth, and delays in implementing new systems negatively affected integration outcomes (Granlund, 2003; Puhakka, 2017). Beusch (2007) identified contradicting management control ideologies in cross-border acquisitions, where employees resisted systems that conflicted with their established ways of working, intensifying ‘we versus they’ dynamics. Jordão, Souza and Avelar (2014) found that control systems played a crucial role in facilitating cultural integration in a Brazilian context, while Puhakka (2017) explored the sensemaking function of accounting systems during PMI, finding that they helped managers and employees interpret the new organizational reality but could also prove inadequate for the demands of integration.

Implementing MCS during PMI presents further challenges. Strategic challenges arise when integration goals are unclear, when strategic misalignment exists between acquirer and target, or when different control elements fail to work cohesively (Beusch, 2007; Nilsson, 2002; Hoque & Chia, 2012). Temporal challenges include delays in implementing new systems, compounded by resource constraints (Granlund, 2003; Puhakka, 2017). Given that each M&A is fundamentally unique, standardized approaches to MCS design are insufficient; control systems require flexible and adaptive components to respond to evolving integration dynamics (Nilsson, 2002; Gates & Very, 2003; Beusch, 2007).

Hoque and Chia (2012) represent the most directly relevant study for the present research, as they explicitly applied Simons’ Levers of Control framework to examine how an Australian manufacturing subsidiary used MCS following its takeover by an overseas parent company. Their study demonstrated that the LOC framework could capture the multi-dimensional nature of control during integration, with different levers serving distinct but complementary functions. However, despite the recognized importance of MCS during integration, research applying comprehensive, multi-dimensional control frameworks to PMI remains limited. Most existing studies examine specific aspects of control, such as management accounting systems or performance measurement, rather than adopting a holistic view of how the full range of control mechanisms operates during integration. Hoque and Chia (2012) represent an exception, but their study focused on a single case. This gap motivates the adoption of Simons’ LOC framework, which provides a comprehensive analytical lens for examining how managers use control systems during PMI.

### Simons’ Levers of Control Framework

The Levers of Control framework was developed by Robert Simons (1994, 1995) based on longitudinal research examining how senior executives use formal management control

systems during strategic transformation. It emerged from studying newly appointed top managers implementing strategies during organizational renewal, focusing on how control systems function not merely for monitoring and constraint but as active levers for strategic change (Simons, 1994, 1995). The framework consists of four interconnected control systems that together help organizations balance innovation and control, managing the tension between creative opportunity-seeking and predictable goal achievement (Simons, 1995). Each lever addresses a distinct organizational variable and serves a specific strategic function.

Belief systems are “the explicit set of organizational definitions that senior managers communicate formally and reinforce systematically to provide basic values, purpose, and direction for the organization” (Simons, 1995, p.40). They inspire and direct the search for new opportunities by communicating core values through mission statements, credos, and vision statements, generating commitment and momentum behind new strategic agendas. Simons (1994) found that newly appointed managers in all cases personally drafted new mission statements within the first six months of their tenure, using belief systems to formalize and communicate their strategic vision.

Boundary systems “delineate the acceptable domain of activity for organizational participants” (Simons, 1995, p.45). They set limits on opportunity-seeking behavior by defining risks to be avoided through codes of business conduct, strategic planning systems, and capital budgeting constraints, providing negative constraints that specify what employees should not do. Simons (1995) compares them to brakes on a car: without them organizations cannot operate at high speed. Boundary systems operate at two levels, strategic boundaries that limit where managers search for opportunities, and business conduct boundaries that prescribe minimum standards of behavior (Simons, 1995; Tessier & Otley, 2012).

Diagnostic control systems are “formal information systems that managers use to monitor organizational outcomes and correct deviations from pre-set standards of performance” (Simons, 1995, p.68). They represent the traditional understanding of management control, focused on measuring critical performance variables such as budgets, profit plans, and balanced scorecards. They enable management by exception, allowing managers to track whether intended strategies are being implemented without continuous personal involvement, and Simons (1994) found managers used them to establish accountability and communicate performance expectations.

Interactive control systems are “formal information systems that managers use to involve themselves regularly and personally in the decision activities of subordinates” (Simons, 1995, p.103). They stimulate organizational learning and the emergence of new strategies by directing attention to strategic uncertainties, the fundamental changes in competitive dynamics, technology, or customer behavior that could disrupt current strategies. They are distinguished by recurring senior-management attention, frequent attention from operating managers, face-to-face interpretation of data, and continual challenge and debate of assumptions and action plans (Simons, 1994, 1995). Unlike diagnostic systems, which operate on an exception basis, interactive systems require active, personal engagement.

Balance among the levers is a fundamental principle of the framework. No single lever is sufficient on its own: belief systems must be balanced with boundary systems to inspire opportunity-seeking while limiting the risk of unbounded exploration, and diagnostic control systems must be balanced with interactive control systems to allow simultaneous exploitation of current strategy and exploration of new opportunities. Simons (1995) characterizes the four levers as generating positive and negative forces that jointly create a dynamic tension between innovation and strategic renewal on one hand and predictable goal achievement on the other. Belief and interactive control systems generate positive forces that expand the scope of organizational activity, while boundary and diagnostic control systems generate negative forces that constrain and focus it. Effective strategic control depends on managing this tension, and the appropriate balance shifts as strategy, industry dynamics, and competitive conditions evolve (Simons, 1995).

### Critique and Development of the Framework

Despite general acceptance in the literature, the Levers of control framework has been criticized for its vague and sometimes ambiguous concept definitions (Ahrens & Chapman, 2004; Bisbe et al., 2007; Ferreira & Otley, 2009). The framework has been cited nearly 800 times, and its strengths, including the inclusion of different types of controls and its broad analytical perspective, are widely acknowledged (Ferreira & Otley, 2009; Tessier & Otley, 2012). However, its main weakness lies in the definitions of its core concepts, which have produced mixed empirical results and made it difficult to compare studies or build a cumulative body of knowledge (Bisbe et al., 2007; Malmi & Brown, 2008).

Tessier and Otley (2012) provide the most comprehensive critique and revision. They identify several ambiguities. First, the concept of interactive control conflates two distinct ideas: the intensity with which managers use controls, and whether the organization has the right strategy in place. Following Ferreira and Otley (2009), they distinguish interactive use of controls, meaning how intensively managers engage with any system, from strategic validity controls, meaning controls that monitor whether the current strategy remains appropriate, and argue that diagnostic and interactive labels describe how control systems are used rather than being control systems in their own right. A budgeting system, for instance, can be used diagnostically, to monitor deviations, or interactively, to debate assumptions and explore new directions, depending on managerial choice.

Second, Tessier and Otley (2012) identify an ambiguity in the relationship between belief and boundary systems. Simons presents these as parallel but opposite levers: beliefs inspire and expand, boundaries constrain and limit. However, Tessier and Otley demonstrate that they operate at different levels of analysis. Belief systems are primarily social controls, encompassing values and norms that can serve both performance and compliance objectives, whereas boundary systems encompass both social and technical controls but are primarily concerned with compliance. In practice, the same organizational value, such as integrity, can

function simultaneously as a belief, inspiring ethical behavior, and a boundary, prohibiting unethical conduct.

Third, Tessier and Otley (2012) argue that boundary systems operate at two distinct levels that Simons identified but did not make sufficiently explicit: strategic boundaries and business conduct boundaries. Combined with the performance/compliance distinction, this produces a revised framework of four control systems, strategic performance, operational performance, strategic boundaries, and operational boundaries, each constituted of social and technical controls and usable diagnostically or interactively.

Tessier and Otley (2012) also distinguish between managerial intentions and employee perceptions, arguing that how employees experience controls may differ significantly from how they are designed. Since this study examines managerial perspectives specifically, the original Simons framework remains the primary analytical lens, with Tessier and Otley’s contributions informing interpretation where relevant.

A second significant critique concerns the concept of balance. Simons (1995) emphasizes that effective strategic control requires balance among the four levers, with positive forces generating dynamic tension against negative ones. However, despite frequent references to balance, Simons does not provide a definitive notion of what balance means in practice or how it is reflected in the configuration of control systems (Kruis, Speklé & Widener, 2016). Kruis et al. (2016) address this gap empirically by surveying 217 business unit managers and using cluster analysis to identify four distinct patterns of balance: strategic vigilance (high scores on all levers, emphasis on diagnostic and boundary controls), strategic exploitation (strong emphasis on boundaries and diagnostic controls with lower reliance on beliefs and interactive systems), strategic responsiveness (high diagnostic and interactive emphasis with relatively weak boundaries), and strategic stability (low scores across all levers). These patterns demonstrate that balance is not a single ideal configuration but takes multiple empirically distinct forms shaped by contextual factors (Kruis et al., 2016). This is important for the present study because it suggests that the way managers configure the four levers during post-merger integration is likely to vary with the specific integration context and conditions involved.

Additional critiques note that the framework focuses primarily on top management, giving insufficient attention to how control systems are experienced at lower organizational levels (Ferreira & Otley, 2009). These limitations are acknowledged, but they do not diminish the framework’s utility for examining how managers design and use control systems during PMI, which is the focus of the present research. The LOC framework remains the most comprehensive and widely applied framework for analyzing the relationship between management control and strategy, and its explicit focus on how managers use control systems as levers of strategic change makes it particularly well-suited to the PMI context.

## Research Method

This study adopts a qualitative research design with an exploratory purpose to investigate how managers use the four levers of control to manage PMI. A qualitative approach is appropriate because the research question asks “how” managers use Simons’ four levers, requiring rich, detailed insights into managerial practices and decision-making rather than statistical measurement or hypothesis testing (Bryman, 2012; Saunders et al., 2023). The exploratory purpose is suitable given that the application of the LOC framework to PMI contexts remains underexplored in existing literature, and the complexity of integration processes, involving cultural differences, organizational restructuring, and competing strategic priorities, demands an approach that allows for flexibility and openness to unexpected findings.

The study seeks to produce knowledge about patterns of meaning and experience rather than quantifiable outcomes. MCS, as described by Simons (1994, 1995), are not standardized technical instruments that can be isolated and measured in uniform terms. They are categorizations of management behavior, encompassing how managers communicate values, set limits, monitor performance, and engage in strategic dialogue. These behaviors are deeply personal, shaped by individual experience, organizational context, and cultural background. Definitions within this domain are inherently imprecise. Even fundamental concepts, such as when an integration process begins and ends, lack clear objective boundaries. A qualitative design accommodates this ambiguity by prioritizing depth of understanding over precision of measurement.

### Research Philosophy

An interpretivist research philosophy underpins this study. Saunders et al. (2023) identify a spectrum of research philosophies, ranging from positivism, which assumes a single observable reality governed by law-like regularities, to interpretivism, which holds that social reality is constructed through the subjective meanings that individuals assign to their experiences. Interpretivism rests on a subjectivist ontology, in which social phenomena are produced through the actions and interpretations of social actors rather than existing independently of them, and an epistemology in which valid knowledge is generated by understanding these meanings rather than by measuring objective regularities (Bryman, 2012; Saunders et al., 2023).

This philosophy is appropriate because MCS are categorizations of management behavior rather than standardized instruments, and their use is mediated by the interpretations and judgments of the managers who deploy them. Understanding how control systems are used therefore requires access to these subjective perspectives, which interpretivism is designed to provide.

### Research Approach

This study follows an abductive approach to theory development. Saunders et al. (2023) distinguish three approaches to the relationship between theory and data: a deductive approach, which begins with theory and tests hypotheses against observations; an inductive approach, which builds theory from observations; and an abductive approach, which combines the two, moving iteratively between theory and data so that theoretical understanding is progressively refined in dialogue with empirical findings. A purely deductive approach would be unsuitable here, as it would treat the four levers as fixed variables to be confirmed or rejected and would not accommodate the exploratory aim of investigating how the levers operate in a context the framework was not designed for.

Simons’ LOC framework therefore provides the deductive starting point. The four levers structure the interview guide, shape the initial analytical categories, and offer a theoretical vocabulary for interpreting findings. However, the framework was not developed with PMI in mind, and interviewees are typically unaware that their management behaviors correspond to specific theoretical categories. The framework is a scholarly categorization of managerial practice, not a set of tools that practitioners consciously select and deploy. This distinction makes a purely deductive application impractical, as the data must be interpreted to identify which managerial practices correspond to each lever rather than simply recording whether predefined controls are present.

The abductive approach allows the study to use the framework as an analytical lens while remaining open to findings that extend, modify, or challenge it. In practice, the research moved iteratively between the framework and the data: the interviews were initially

structured and interpreted through the four levers, but the analysis surfaced patterns the framework did not fully anticipate, prompting a return to the literature to make sense of them. This movement between theory and data, rather than a one-directional test of theory or a theory-free exploration, is the defining characteristic of the abductive approach and reflects how theoretical understanding developed through the study.

### Methodological Choice

This study adopts a mono-method qualitative design, employing a single qualitative data collection technique, semi-structured interviews, with a corresponding qualitative analysis. Saunders et al. (2023) distinguish mono-method designs from multi-method designs, which combine several techniques within a single methodological approach, and mixed-method designs, which integrate qualitative and quantitative techniques. A mono-method qualitative design was selected because the research question seeks depth of understanding rather than breadth of measurement, and a single, consistently applied technique allows the study to explore managerial practices in detail across all participants.

### Research Strategy

This study adopts a qualitative interview study as its research strategy, collecting individual accounts from practitioners with direct PMI experience and analyzing them for patterns using Simons’ LOC framework as the analytical lens. Eight semi-structured interviews were conducted with professionals working in different capacities within PMI, including integration managers, integration planners, and integration consultants who advise organizations through the process. The interviewees were employed at different organizations at the time of the interviews, and their experiences spanned multiple integration contexts rather than being confined to a single transaction or company.

This design was chosen over a case study strategy because the aim is not to produce a detailed account of one or two integrations but to gather diverse perspectives and identify recurring patterns across multiple integration contexts (Bryman, 2012; Saunders et al., 2023). The inclusion of participants from different roles, both internal leads and external consultants, provides a broader view than a single-organization study would permit.

Semi-structured interviews were selected as the primary data collection method because they offer the flexibility required to explore how managers interpret and use control systems in practice. Since each participant had been involved in different types of integrations and held different roles, the ability to adapt questions and pursue relevant follow-ups was essential, allowing deep exploration when an interviewee provided an unexpected or particularly rich response while still ensuring that all interviews covered the same core areas grounded in the four levers (Bryman, 2012). A fully structured interview would not have allowed this

adaptation, while a fully unstructured approach would have risked losing the theoretical focus provided by Simons’ framework, making systematic comparison across interviews difficult.

The interview guide was structured around the four levers but translated into accessible, non-academic language, addressing practical realities rather than theoretical categories. Questions were framed in the third person (‘How did the management team…’) rather than the second person, reducing social desirability bias and acknowledging that integration involves multiple actors across organizational levels.

### Time Horizon

This study adopts a cross-sectional time horizon, examining the phenomenon at a single point in time rather than tracking it over an extended period. Saunders et al. (2023) distinguish cross-sectional studies, which capture a snapshot of a phenomenon at a particular moment, from longitudinal studies, which observe change and development over time. The interviews were conducted across several months, but each captured the participant’s reflections at the time of speaking rather than following any single integration as it unfolded.

A cross-sectional horizon is appropriate given the exploratory purpose of the study, since the aim is to understand how managers use the four levers during integration based on their experience, not to observe the evolution of control system use across the phases of a specific integration in real time. Because PMI unfolds over months or years and the use of control systems is likely to shift as it progresses, this design relies on participants’ retrospective accounts and cannot capture that evolution directly, a limitation considered further in the limitations chapter (7.1).

### Data Collection and Sampling

The study employed purposive sampling to identify participants with direct and relevant experience of PMI. Purposive sampling involves the deliberate selection of participants who meet predefined criteria relevant to the research question, rather than relying on random or convenience-based selection (Bryman, 2012; Saunders et al., 2023). The primary selection criterion was demonstrable experience within mergers and acquisitions, specifically the PMI phase, with preference given to individuals in management or integration-leading roles. This ensured that participants could speak with authority about how control systems are used during integration.

Participant recruitment began through the researcher’s personal network but yielded limited results, after which recruitment shifted primarily to LinkedIn. Using the platform’s filtering capabilities, individuals with M&A and PMI experience were identified and contacted

through direct outreach. A degree of snowball sampling also occurred through recommendations from previous contacts.

Eight interviews were conducted in total. The participants occupied a range of roles within PMI, including integration managers, integration planners, and integration consultants. The sample spanned both internal and external perspectives: the majority had experience leading or planning integrations within organizations, while a smaller number were currently working as consultants advising other firms through the integration process, and most participants had some exposure to consulting work over the course of their careers. All interviews were conducted via video conferencing and were audio recorded with the participants’ consent. Interviews typically lasted approximately one hour, with one interview extending beyond two hours. Data collection took place over a period of several months. Seven of the eight interviews were conducted in Swedish, with one conducted in English; all quotations presented in this dissertation have been translated into English, with care taken to preserve the meaning and nuance of the original responses.

Recordings were transcribed using a locally installed artificial intelligence transcription tool, operated offline with no internet connection in order to safeguard the confidentiality of the data. Each transcription was subsequently reviewed manually to correct errors, ensure accuracy, and confirm comprehension of the content. Anonymization was carried out during the transcription process, also offline, with participant and organizational identifiers replaced with pseudonyms and, subsequently, all company references replaced with the placeholder “[Company].” The handling of confidential data and the anonymization procedure are discussed further in the Ethical Considerations section.

No secondary data was collected alongside the interviews. Participants did not provide prior material identifying the specific integrations they referred to, and the majority of the cases discussed fall under strict non-disclosure agreements or confidentiality obligations. Consequently, no company-specific secondary sources such as internal integration plans or confidential reports could be obtained or are presented in this study. The analysis therefore rests entirely on the accounts provided by participants during the interviews.

### Data Analysis

The interview data was analyzed using thematic analysis, an approach for identifying, analyzing, and reporting patterns within qualitative data (Braun & Clarke, 2006). Thematic analysis is well suited to this study because it accommodates both theory-driven and data-driven analysis, aligning with the abductive approach adopted throughout the research. The four levers of control provided the initial analytical structure, while the analysis remained open to themes that emerged from the data and were not anticipated by the framework.

The analysis followed a deductive-then-inductive logic. The interview guide was deliberately structured so that its questions addressed the practical realities underlying each of the four levers, which meant that the data was organized around these theoretical categories from the point of collection. In the first stage of analysis, responses were therefore grouped according to the lever they related to, providing a deductive framework derived from Simons’ (1995) theory. In the second stage, the data within and across these categories was examined inductively to identify recurring patterns, points of agreement and divergence between participants, and themes that did not fit neatly within the existing framework.

The analysis was conducted in Swedish, the language in which seven of the eight interviews were carried out, in order to preserve the meaning and nuance of participants’ responses and to avoid the loss of detail that can occur through premature translation. Only the final quotations selected for presentation in the dissertation were translated into English, with attention paid to retaining the intended meaning of the original statements. Analyzing the data in its original language reduced the risk of misinterpretation and ensured that the patterns identified reflected what participants actually expressed rather than artifacts of translation.

### Research Quality and Trustworthiness

Quality in qualitative research is assessed differently from quantitative research, where validity and reliability are the central criteria. Because this study adopts an interpretivist philosophy and does not seek statistical generalization, its quality is evaluated using the criteria of trustworthiness: credibility, transferability, dependability, and confirmability

(Bryman, 2012; Saunders et al., 2023). Each criterion was addressed through specific measures taken during the design and conduct of the study.

Credibility concerns the extent to which the findings accurately represent the participants’ accounts and experiences. Conducting the analysis in the original language of the interviews preserved the meaning and nuance of participants’ responses, transcriptions were reviewed manually against the recordings to ensure accuracy, and the inclusion of participants from a range of roles, both those who had led integrations internally and those who had advised on them externally, provided a degree of source triangulation, allowing patterns that recurred across different vantage points to be identified with greater confidence.

Transferability concerns the extent to which the findings can be applied to other contexts. Qualitative research of this kind does not aim for the statistical generalization associated with quantitative studies; instead, it seeks analytical generalization, in which findings contribute to theoretical understanding that may be relevant in comparable settings (Yin, 2014; Saunders et al., 2023). To support transferability, the study provides a detailed description of its context, including the nature of the participants, their roles, and the integration settings they described, in so far as confidentiality permits. This contextual detail allows readers to assess the extent to which the findings may be applicable to other organizations, industries, or national settings.

Dependability concerns whether the research process is consistent and could be followed and understood by others. This was supported by the transparent documentation of all methodological decisions, from the sampling strategy and interview design through to the analytical process. The chapter provides a clear account of how the data moved from raw interviews through transcription, coding against the four levers, and the identification of cross-interview patterns, establishing an audit trail that makes the basis for the findings traceable.

Confirmability concerns the extent to which the findings are shaped by the data rather than by the researcher’s own assumptions or biases. Complete objectivity is not claimed, as it is incompatible with the interpretivist position adopted here; rather, the aim is to demonstrate that the findings are grounded in what participants expressed. This was supported by grounding the findings in participants’ own accounts and by reflexivity regarding the researcher’s position. The researcher’s use of a personal network and familiarity with the field provided valuable access but also carried the potential for bias, and maintaining awareness of this throughout the analysis helped ensure the interpretations remained grounded in the data rather than in prior expectations.

While these measures strengthen the trustworthiness of the study, certain limitations remain inherent to the chosen design, including the subjectivity introduced by an interpretivist approach, the reliance on self-reported data, and the constraints of a small sample examined at a single point in time. These limitations are considered in detail in the concluding chapter, 7.1.

### Ethical Considerations

Ethical considerations were given particular attention throughout this study, given the sensitive nature of the information discussed and the confidentiality obligations surrounding many of the integrations participants referred to. The study was conducted in accordance with established principles of research ethics, including informed consent, voluntary participation, anonymity, and the secure handling of data (Bryman, 2012; Saunders et al., 2023).

Prior to each interview, participants were provided with documentation outlining the purpose of the study, how the data would be used, and their rights as participants, including the right to withdraw. On this basis, informed consent was obtained verbally before each interview commenced, and participants consented to the interviews being audio recorded. Following each interview, the resulting transcription was made available to the participant, ensuring transparency regarding how their contribution had been recorded and interpreted.

Anonymization was a central ethical requirement, both to protect participants and to respect the non-disclosure agreements and confidentiality obligations attached to many of the cases discussed. Anonymization was carried out during the transcription process, with participant and organizational identifiers replaced with pseudonyms. All references to specific companies were subsequently replaced with the placeholder “[Company]”, and other identifying details, including national and contextual particulars where these could be used to identify an organization, were generalized or removed. The standard applied was that no participant or organization should be identifiable from the material presented, while retaining sufficient contextual information to preserve the analytical value of the data.

The confidentiality of the data was further protected through secure handling procedures. Transcription was performed using a locally installed tool operated offline, with no internet connection, in order to prevent any possibility of confidential data being transmitted or stored externally. Recording and identifying material were stored securely and will be deleted following the completion and assessment of the dissertation, in line with data protection principles and the expectations communicated to participants. Given the commercial sensitivity of merger and acquisition activity, particular care was taken throughout to ensure that no confidential business information could be traced to any specific individual, organization, or transaction.

## Empirical Findings

This chapter presents the findings from the eight interviews. It is organized around the four levers of control, beginning with belief systems, followed by boundary systems, diagnostic control systems, and interactive control systems, and concludes with a section on themes that emerged from the data beyond the framework. The findings are presented descriptively, with

participants’ accounts illustrated through translated quotations and attributed to participants by the identifiers introduced in the preceding chapter.

### Belief Systems

Belief systems emerged as the most consistently and confidently described lever across the eight interviews. Where later questions about boundaries or measurement often produced hesitation, participants spoke about values, vision, and communication readily and at length, and a clear majority treated this lever as decisive for a successful integration. The strength of agreement is itself the first pattern worth noting: there was little dispute about whether belief systems mattered, only variation in how they were enacted.

The dominant theme within this lever was communication. Participants returned repeatedly to the idea that strong, early, and continuous communication shapes the trajectory of an integration, and several connected the quality of that communication directly to whether the acquired organization came to trust the acquirer. Several framed the underlying problem in emotional rather than procedural terms: acquired employees enter integration uncertain about their roles and their futures, and that uncertainty, if left unaddressed, makes subsequent steps harder. Communication was therefore described less as the transmission of information and more as a way of managing uncertainty and building trust.

A set of practices described by participants shared this same function: welcome kits, invitations to conferences, introductory days, shared leadership training, and physical visits to acquired sites. Although these vary in form, participants presented them as ways of signaling inclusion and making people from the acquired organization feel part of a single combined entity rather than absorbed as an afterthought. Grouped together, they represent variations on a common behavior of reducing the sense of distance and threat that an acquisition creates, and this behavior recurred across participants who otherwise described very different integrations.

A second pattern concerns who supplied the values. Several buyer-side participants described acquired firms that had no clearly articulated values or vision of their own, and in those cases the acquirer supplied its own. One participant explained that “the acquired company had no clearly evaluated or stated values or vision, mission, so we took the values we had as a company and packaged them in their language” (P2), translating the acquirer’s existing values into the local language before distributing them. This sits alongside the view, expressed by other participants, that vision and purpose should be aligned between buyer and seller before signing. In the accounts given, alignment was rarely a meeting of two fully formed value sets; more often the acquirer’s values filled a vacuum, or had to be argued for so that the acquired organization could see itself within them. Participants who spoke about pre-signing alignment described treating a genuine mismatch of purpose as a reason for caution rather than an automatic reason to abandon a deal.

Who delivered the message also emerged as a recurring point. Participants indicated that the identity of the messenger mattered, and that the most senior figures were expected to carry it. The chief executive of the acquiring company was frequently the visible voice, even where the wording and method were designed by communication specialists. Several participants also noted that where the acquired organization held strong trust in its own existing leadership, that leader sometimes needed to be the initial messenger, and that a message carried jointly by both chief executives could ease the handover more smoothly than a message delivered from the acquiring side alone.

A further pattern concerns the relationship between stated and lived values. Several participants stressed that communicated values needed to be genuine and capable of being upheld, rather than chosen to match what employees were assumed to want to hear. Two participants, describing separate integrations, illustrated opposite outcomes. One, speaking from the acquiring side, described a vision that was not only communicated but materialized over time, observing with satisfaction that “the visions that were communicated have actually come true; it was not just on paper, it became that vision too” (P1). The other, observing a different integration from the acquired and product side, described a visible disconnect between projection and experience, where “the hallway conversations in front of those placards were that people were not buying in, a disconnect between what the company projected and what people lived” (P6). Across the interviews, participants positioned on the acquired side or observing externally more often described belief systems as fragile or surface-level, while those speaking from a position of control over the integration more often described them as working as intended.

### Boundary Systems

Boundary systems were the lever that participants described least readily. The contrast with belief systems was marked; the same participants who spoke at length and with confidence about values and communication became noticeably more hesitant, brief, or uncertain when asked directly about limits, rules, and what was off limits during integration. This difference in how the two levers were discussed, within the same interviews, is the first and clearest pattern in the boundary data. Boundaries were almost always present in some form, but they were rarely something participants articulated spontaneously or in detail.

When asked directly what was off limits and whether anyone had crossed a line, participants frequently gave diffuse or minimal answers, often after visible hesitation. In several cases the response amounted to there having been no real boundaries to speak of; one participant concluded that “nothing at all, actually, not in this case; there were not really any boundaries anyone could step over” (P1), reasoning that the acquiring organization was so dominant in size and its guidelines so clear that there were no lines left to cross. In accounts of this type, boundaries were treated as settled in advance through the acquisition agreement rather than as

something actively managed during integration, existing as static contractual facts rather than as a lever managers worked with.

Where participants did not give a thin answer, they tended to redirect the question onto adjacent territory rather than address behavioral limits directly. Three forms of redirection recurred. The most common was to reframe boundaries as legal and regulatory compliance, describing limits as a matter of operating correctly within laws, agreements, and rules. A second was to reframe boundaries as commercial or operational risk, where the relevant limit became the risk of the business failing to function rather than a rule governing conduct. A third was to reframe the question as a resourcing conflict; one participant, asked about sacred cows and crossing lines, described instead a dispute in which the acquired organization felt deprioritized when technical resources were directed toward existing customers (P5). The common thread across these responses is that boundaries were interpreted as something concrete and external rather than as internal limits on behavior.

A further pattern concerns the timing of boundary setting. Rather than being established up front, boundaries often appeared only after a problem had surfaced. One participant described “a fairly low degree of performance management; if someone did something wrong it was not as if they got a reprimand” (P2), with non-compliance going largely unaddressed and issues carried from meeting to meeting, until a more structured control mechanism in the form of a detailed checklist was introduced later, once the absence of such control had become a recognized problem. The pattern across several accounts is of boundary control tightening reactively in response to difficulty rather than being designed in advance.

Two participants stood out from the diffuse middle, in opposite ways. One, the only participant interviewed in English and describing an integration in North America, gave by far the most explicit boundary account of the eight, describing named limits on the product side, decisions about what was off limits taken at senior executive level, and “a formal review of sales and transactions to make sure the product was being used in ways the company could stand behind” (P6), which became more important after the merger. The second stood out not for the clarity of formal boundaries but for describing the most active boundary trouble: explicit conflicts during the execution phase in which people did what suited them, handled through escalation up to top management in a steering group structure (P8). Together these two accounts bracket the pattern, one describing the most structured boundaries and the other the most visible boundary failures, with most participants falling somewhere less defined between them.

### Diagnostic Control Systems

Diagnostic control systems were present in a clear majority of the accounts and were treated by every participant as a necessary part of integration. In some interviews the lever was described only briefly, but on close reading this brevity reflected established and assumed measurement practices rather than their absence. Several participants appeared to treat

diagnostic control as self-evident, sometimes so obviously a part of running an integration that it did not require elaboration, and the substance of their measurement practices was evident even where the answers themselves were short.

What participants tracked converged on a common core despite the variety of industries represented. Exact metrics were never disclosed, but a majority described monitoring measures of sales and revenue, with each industry and business type using its own specific figures to capture what amounted to the same underlying performance. Several participants also referred to milestones and targets set at the outset of the integration, though these were described less uniformly and were not a feature of every account. The consistency lay less in the particular numbers than in what the numbers were intended to reflect, which was broadly shared across otherwise dissimilar integrations.

A recurring rhythm structured this monitoring. The most common reporting cycle described was monthly, with several participants noting larger reviews held quarterly, and all indicating that some form of structured check took place at least once a month. One participant captured the structured cadence in describing how “the business case was followed up monthly; we went through part by part and saw what happened, what we had learned, how it affected our forecasts” (P8). In at least one account this formal cadence sat above a faster informal layer, as another participant noted that “in the integration project we had monthly measures, but the deliverables were sometimes steered day by day and week by week” (P5). Finance frequently functioned as part of this monitoring, with monthly figures serving as an early indication of where an integration was deviating from expectation.

Several participants acknowledged that the formal numbers tended to lag behind the actual progress of the integration, as the work of integrating moved faster than monthly reporting could capture. This lag was not generally presented as a failing. Participants who raised it described having accounted for it, treating the delays as a known characteristic of the data that was factored into how figures were interpreted during reviews rather than as a problem that caught them unprepared. In the accounts that combined a monthly reporting cycle with faster day-to-day or weekly tracking, the slower formal metrics and the quicker operational follow-up appeared to function together rather than in competition.

Synergies emerged as the most contested aspect of this lever. Participants consistently described synergy as one of the most difficult elements of integration to handle diagnostically, but they diverged on why. Some doubted that synergies could be realized in the way they are commonly imagined at the point of acquisition, one stating plainly that “I do not believe in synergies generally” (P2), while others considered them achievable but very hard to measure and to fully complete. Even among those who believed in synergies, the difficulty of isolating and quantifying them was a shared theme, and skepticism about whether projected synergies would materialize as planned recurred across several accounts.

### Interactive Control System

Interactive control systems were described by every participant, but with the least consistency of the four levers. No participant found the lever difficult to explain, yet the accounts varied considerably in form and emphasis, with each describing a somewhat different approach to how managers involved themselves in the integration. The one element common to all accounts was the use of meetings as a forum for interaction and for following the progress of the integration, whether held weekly, monthly, or quarterly. Beyond the meetings themselves, many participants also described one-on-one conversations and informal exchanges used to share information outside the formal forums, indicating that interaction took place through several channels rather than a single mechanism.

In practice, the most frequently described forms were steering groups and regular project meetings, which functioned as the formal backbone of interaction, alongside a less formal layer of one-on-one and direct conversation. One participant described problems typically beginning in a one-on-one when an individual raised an issue, which then moved into a working meeting of the most senior people in the relevant area, and on occasion into workshops and shared meals intended to rebuild relationships. Others centered their accounts on a recurring weekly forum, whether a steering group or a cross-functional project meeting, in which the integration was reviewed and decisions were taken. Across these descriptions, the formal forum and the informal relational layer were presented as working together, with conversation feeding into the structured meetings. One participant characterized the underlying work as “very much about asking the right questions; then you see something is not moving and you contact people” (P7).

A recurring pattern concerned how this interaction changed over time. Several participants described interactive forums being most intensive at the start of an integration and becoming less frequent as it progressed. One described steering group meetings held weekly during the first six months to enable rapid decisions, with each workstream reporting upward, and the cadence then easing to biweekly, monthly, and quarterly as the integration matured. This pattern of an intensive early period settling into a slower rhythm recurred across several accounts.

Participants frequently emphasized the personal involvement of senior figures in these forums. The presence of chief executives and finance officers was described as significant rather than incidental, and one participant valued “having the chief executive and finance chief involved; they get two inputs” (P5), gaining two distinct perspectives at once. In other accounts, matters that could not be resolved at lower levels were escalated upward to top management within the steering structure, again placing senior figures directly in the interactive process rather than receiving its outputs at a remove.

A particularly distinctive pattern concerned how problems came to light. Several participants described issues surfacing through people and conversation before they appeared in the formal data. One noted that crises often became visible through salespeople raising delivery

and customer problems before the lagging figures reflected them. Another, observing from the acquired and product side, described tracing anomalies in the numbers back to a human source, arguing that not all relevant information takes the form of data. A third described deliberately cultivating openness, explaining that “it was my job to create a culture where you dared to speak up when you saw problems; you were not punished, rather praised for detecting as early as possible” (P8). Together these accounts present the interactive layer as an early-warning mechanism operating ahead of the diagnostic figures.

One participant drew attention to the limits of relying on this human channel, describing concerns traveling through one-on-ones and an informal chain that depended on individuals remembering and acting on what they heard, with the result that information could be lost, and observing that leadership tended to place greater trust in data than in the human voice. This was the clearest account of the interactive channel as imperfect, in contrast to the more confident descriptions given by other participants.

### Themes Beyond the Framework

Alongside the four levers, several themes recurred across the interviews that did not fall neatly within any single lever but bear directly on how managers approached control during integration. These themes emerged from the data rather than from the framework and are presented descriptively.

The first concerns a widespread skepticism toward synergies. Although synergies are typically central to the rationale for an acquisition, several participants expressed doubt about whether they are realized as intended or can be meaningfully measured. The views ranged from outright doubt that synergies materialize in the way they are imagined at the point of acquisition, to a more qualified position that they are achievable but very difficult to isolate and complete. This skepticism cut across participants on both the acquiring side and the advisory side, and it shaped how they regarded the value of measuring integration progress against projected synergy targets.

A second theme concerns the reliability of the information available before integration begins. Several participants questioned the adequacy of due diligence, describing it as presenting an idealized picture rather than an accurate one, and as being conducted by people removed from the operational reality they were assessing. One participant invoked a common expression to capture this, describing how due diligence tends to show the version of a company the seller wishes to present (P8). A related observation came from the experience of carve-outs, where what is agreed in principle before signing turns out to be far harder to deliver afterward, leaving integration teams to manage commitments that prove unworkable in practice (P5). In both forms, the theme is that managers frequently entered integration working from information that could not be fully trusted.

A third strongly recurring theme is the view that the central difficulty of integration is human rather than technical. Several participants argued that systems, processes, and structures are comparatively straightforward to combine, and that the persistent challenge lies in people, relationships, and behavior. This view was expressed both by consultants reflecting across many integrations and by participants describing specific cases, and it was frequently tied to the argument that the soft, human dimension of integration is routinely underestimated relative to the operational and financial dimension. The theme aligns with the weight participants placed on belief systems and interactive control, and with the comparative difficulty they had in articulating formal boundaries.

## Discussion

### The Use and Balance of the Four Levers

The findings indicate that the four levers of control are not used equally during post-merger integration, and that the imbalance follows a discernible pattern. Belief systems and interactive control systems were the most actively and richly engaged levers across the interviews. Diagnostic control systems were universally presented but were treated as routine, almost as infrastructure that did not require much elaboration. Boundary systems were the least developed, both in how often they were used as an active management tool and in how readily participants were able to articulate them. The picture that emerges is therefore not one of balanced reliance on all four levers, but of a weighting tilted toward some levers and away from others.

This weighting maps closely onto Simons’ (1995) distinction between positive and negative forces. Simons argues that belief and interactive control systems act as positive forces that inspire, expand, and direct the search for opportunity, while boundary and diagnostic control systems act as negative forces that constrain and focus organizational behavior, and that the two sets generate a dynamic tension that effective control must balance. In the present findings, the two levers used most actively, belief and interactive systems, are precisely Simons’ positive forces, while the lever that was weakest, boundary systems, is one of his negative forces. The integrations described by participants thus appear to have leaned heavily on the expansive, motivating forces of control while making comparatively limited active use of the constraining force.

This interpretation requires one important qualification. Diagnostic control systems are also a negative force in Simons’ scheme, yet they were not weak in the way boundary systems were. They were consistently present and accepted as a necessary part of integration. The difference is that diagnostic control was treated as established and routine rather than as something managers actively shaped, whereas boundary systems were genuinely underdeveloped. The constraining side of the framework was therefore not uniformly weak. Rather, the weakness was concentrated specifically in boundary systems, while diagnostic

measurement continued to function in the background. This distinction matters, because it suggests that the imbalance was not simply a general aversion to control, but something more specific to the setting of explicit limits and rules.

The variation between participants was also revealing, and differed by lever. Boundary systems were thin across almost all participants, with the clearest exception being the participant whose account was rooted furthest from the Nordic management context, which points toward a contextual or cultural influence on this lever. Interactive control systems, by contrast, varied in a different way: while all participants used the lever, the form it took differed considerably from one account to the next, from highly structured weekly forums to more informal and conversational approaches. This suggests that the levers are not all shaped by the same forces. Boundary systems appear sensitive to the cultural context in which a manager operates, while interactive control systems appear more sensitive to the individual style of the manager using them. The levers therefore differ not only in how heavily they are used, but in what most strongly influences how they are used, a point developed further in the sections that follow.

### Boundary Systems, Culture, and the Substitution of Belief for Boundary

The most distinctive finding of this study is the consistent weakness of boundary systems, and it is also the finding that most clearly invites interpretation beyond Simons’ framework. Across the interviews, participants found boundary systems difficult to articulate, frequently reframing questions about limits and rules as matters of legal compliance, operational risk, or resourcing, and in several cases describing no meaningful limits at all. This was not a failure of the participants to understand the question, nor an absence of control in their integrations. It is better understood as an indication that, in this setting, explicit boundaries were simply not a prominent part of how managers thought about steering integration.

A plausible explanation for this pattern lies in the national cultural context shared by the majority of participants. Hofstede’s model of national culture characterizes countries along several dimensions that capture how societies handle authority, uncertainty, and social relations, and that have been widely used to explain differences in management practice across countries (Hofstede, Hofstede & Minkov, 2010). Three of these dimensions are particularly relevant to the present finding. Sweden scores very low on uncertainty avoidance, indicating a cultural comfort with ambiguity and a correspondingly weak felt need to formalize rules in advance. It scores low on power distance, reflecting flat hierarchies and consensus-oriented relationships in which top-down rule setting may be experienced as unnecessarily authoritarian. It also scores at the feminine end of the masculinity-femininity dimension, since renamed motivation toward achievement and success, indicating an emphasis on cooperation, relationships, and quality of life rather than on assertiveness, competition, and structured control (Hofstede, Hofstede & Minkov, 2010; The Culture Factor

Group, 2024 ). Taken together, these three dimensions describe a cultural setting in which explicit, formalized boundaries are likely to feel both unnecessary and out of keeping with prevailing management norms, which corresponds closely to what participants described.

This cultural reading is consistent with the one participant whose account stood apart. The participant whose integration was rooted furthest from the Nordic management context described by far the most explicit boundary system, including formal review of sales and transactions and clearer defined limits. While a single contrasting case cannot establish a cultural explanation on its own, it is consistent with the interpretation that the weakness of boundary systems observed elsewhere is shaped by cultural context rather than being an intrinsic feature of post-merger integration.

The weakness of boundary systems does not, however, mean that the function those systems are intended to serve was absent. Simons (1995) presents boundary systems as the lever that defines the limits of acceptable behavior and keeps opportunity-seeking within safe bounds. In the integrations described here, that function appears to have been carried less by explicit rules than by shared values and trust. Several participants described relying on a strong, clearly communicated culture to keep behavior within acceptable limits, with one stating that a strong culture made formal limits unnecessary because anyone who did not fit would simply not remain. In other words, the work that boundary systems formally perform in Simons’ framework appears, in this context, to have been performed substantially by belief systems.

This observation connects directly to Tessier and Otley’s (2012) critique of the framework. They argue that belief and boundary systems are not as separate as Simons presents them, and that the same underlying value can function simultaneously as a belief that inspires and as a boundary that constrains. The present findings provide empirical support for this argument and extend it: in a cultural setting where explicit boundaries are culturally disfavored, belief systems do not merely overlap with boundary systems but appear to take over much of their function. This suggests that the apparent weakness of one lever should not be read in isolation, because the control that a weak lever would formally provide may be supplied by another. It also suggests that the balance Simons describes between positive and negative forces can be achieved through configurations he did not explicitly anticipate, with a positive force compensating for an underused negative one.

### Patterns of Balance

The balance observed across the interviews can also be considered in relation to Kruis, Speklé and Widener’s (2016) empirical work on how organizations combine the four levers. Rather than treating balance as a single ideal configuration, Kruis et al. identified several distinct patterns in which organizations weight the levers differently, shaped by their context and circumstances. Among these, the pattern that places relatively strong emphasis on the more adaptive and dialogue-oriented levers while making comparatively limited use of

formal boundaries offers a useful point of comparison for the present findings. The integrations described here, with their heavy reliance on belief and interactive systems and their notably weak use of boundary systems, appear to resemble this kind of configuration more closely than one built around tight, rule-based control.

This resemblance is suggestive rather than definitive; the study was not designed to classify integrations into balance patterns in the way Kruis, Speklé and Widener’s (2016) survey-based approach allowed. Nonetheless, a profile combining strong adaptive control with weak formal boundaries has been observed empirically elsewhere, which lends some support to the view that the boundary weakness found here represents a recognizable lever configuration rather than a deficiency.

A further consideration is that the setting itself may encourage this kind of balance. Kruis et al. studied established business units operating under relatively stable conditions, whereas the present study concerns organizations in the midst of integration, a period defined by disruption, uncertainty, and the need to adapt continually as circumstances change. It seems reasonable to suggest that such conditions would naturally favor the more flexible, dialogue-oriented levers, which allow managers to respond to emerging issues, over rigid boundary systems that fix limits in advance. If so, the weak boundary systems observed here may reflect not only the cultural context discussed earlier but also the particular demands of the integration period itself. These two influences are not mutually exclusive; the cultural context and the nature of integration may reinforce one another, both pointing managers toward adaptive rather than constraining forms of control. This interpretation is offered tentatively, as the present study cannot separate the influence of culture from the influence of the integration setting, but it suggests that the balance observed here is plausibly shaped by both.

### Interactive Control and the Influence of Individual Style

While boundary systems appeared sensitive to the cultural context in which managers operated, interactive control systems varied in a different way. All participants used this lever, and most regarded it as central to managing integration, but the form it took differed considerably from one account to the next. Some described highly structured arrangements built around regular steering groups and formal forums, while others relied more on informal conversation, one-on-one contact, and direct personal involvement. Unlike boundary systems, where the variation pointed toward a shared cultural influence, the variation in interactive control did not appear to follow national or contextual lines so much as the individual approach of the manager describing it.

This raises the possibility that interactive control systems are shaped less by the cultural setting of an integration than by the personality of the manager using them. Where belief systems were communicated through broadly similar channels and boundary systems were consistently thin, the way managers chose to involve themselves in the day-to-day work of

integration seemed to reflect their own preferences for how to engage with people. A manager inclined toward direct, frequent personal contact would naturally use the interactive lever differently from one who preferred to work through structured meetings and formal reporting. The lever provides a wide range of possible forms, and which of those forms a manager adopts may depend substantially on the kind of person they are.

This interpretation is offered with caution; the study did not measure individual disposition, and participants describing their own behavior may characterize it differently from how others would. What can be said is that the variation in this lever was notably individual in character, in a way that variation in the other levers was not, and that this difference invites further consideration.

### Relation to Prior Research and Contribution

The study that most directly informs the present research is Hoque and Chia (2012), who applied Simons’ levers of control framework to a single Australian manufacturing subsidiary following its takeover by an overseas parent company. Their study found that the four levers worked together in tandem to support strategy implementation during the change that followed the takeover, with belief and interactive systems driving the search for opportunity and diagnostic and boundary systems providing focus and control. The present findings are broadly consistent with this in one important respect: the four levers were all present in the integration described here, and belief and interactive systems were again the most prominent in driving the integration forward. Where the present study departs from Hoque and Chia is in what it reveals about how the levers relate to one another, and why.

Hoque and Chia described a case in which boundary systems were clearly developed and articulated, including defined product-market boundaries, codes of conduct, and explicitly stated rules of acceptable behavior. The present study, by contrast, found boundary systems to be consistently weak and difficult for participants to articulate. Because Hoque and Chia examined a single organization, their study could not reveal whether the configuration they observed was typical or shaped by context. By drawing on multiple participants across different integrations who nonetheless shared a common cultural context, the present study observes that boundary systems were not merely underdeveloped in one organization but consistently so across many. The contrast with Hoque and Chia’s well-developed boundary systems, in a setting outside the Nordic context, is consistent with a contextual rather than an organization-specific explanation.

The present study also extends Hoque and Chia’s central finding. Where they showed that levers work together in tandem, the findings here suggest that the lever can do more than complement one another: under certain conditions one lever may substantially take over the function of another, as belief systems appear to have done in place of boundary systems. This moves beyond the idea of complementarity toward a notion of substitution, in which the balance Simons describes is maintained not by all four levers operating actively, but by a

strongly used lever compensating for a weakly used one. This is a contribution that a single-case design is not well positioned to surface, because it depends on observing the same imbalance recurring across multiple settings.

Taken together, the contribution of this study is threefold. Empirically, it extends the application of the levers of control framework from single cases toward a broader set of practitioners and integrations, including those crossing national borders. Theoretically, it suggests that the relationship between the levers includes substitution as well as complementarity, and that the balance between them is shaped by national cultural context. And in relation to existing literature, it indicates that the configuration of the levers observed in a single non-Nordic case cannot be assumed to generalize, since the present findings, drawn from a different cultural setting, differ from that configuration in a consistent and theoretically meaningful way.

## Conclusions

This study set out to explore how managers use Simons’ four levers of control during post-merger integration. Drawing on eight interviews with managers and consultants experienced in integration, it examined how belief, boundary, diagnostic, and interactive control systems were used in practice, and what shaped that use.

The central conclusion is that the four levers are not used equally during integration, and that the imbalance is patterned rather than incidental. Belief systems and interactive control systems were the most actively and confidently used, while boundary systems were consistently the weakest and least clearly articulated, and diagnostic control systems were present but treated as routine. In Simons’ terms, the positive forces of control dominated while one of the negative forces, boundary systems, was notably underdeveloped. The study further found that different levers appear to be shaped by different influences: boundary systems by the cultural context in which managers operate, and interactive control systems by the individual approach of the manager using them. The answer to the research question is therefore not simply that managers use all four levers, but that they lean heavily on the expansive, communicative levers while making limited active use of formal constraint, in a configuration that reflects both the cultural setting and the disruptive nature of integration.

The study makes three contributions. Theoretically, it extends the application of the levers of control framework, previously applied to single cases of post-merger integration, to a broader set of managers across multiple integrations, and in doing so suggests two refinements to how the framework is understood in this context. The first is that the levers can substitute for one another rather than only complementing one another, with belief systems appearing to take over much of the function that boundary systems formally perform, extending Tessier and Otley’s (2012) argument that belief and boundary systems overlap. The second is that the balance between the levers is shaped by national cultural context, which Simons’ framework

acknowledges only in general terms. Empirically, the study contributes insights drawn directly from managers and consultants with first-hand integration experience, including the consistent finding that boundary systems are difficult to articulate and lightly used in a Nordic management context. Practically, it offers guidance for managers leading integration, developed below.

The framework largely holds up: the four levers were identifiable in practice and provided a workable structure for understanding integration, which supports their continued relevance. What the findings suggest needs adjustment is the treatment of the levers as a balanced set of complementary mechanisms. Balance was achieved here through compensation rather than uniform activity, and the weighting between levers was shaped by cultural factors the framework does not foreground. Boundary systems in particular may carry an implicit assumption of conditions, higher power distance, a greater tendency to formalize rules, that are not universally present. Where those conditions are weaker, the boundary lever is not simply used less but substituted for by belief systems. This points less to a rejection of Simons than to a more flexible reading in which levers substitute as well as complement, and cultural context shapes which are treated as necessary at all.

The central practical implication concerns the relationship between belief systems and formal boundaries. Because shared values appeared to carry much of the constraining work that explicit boundaries formally perform, managers should recognize that thin formal rules during integration do not necessarily indicate a lack of control. Where a strong, genuinely held culture is in place, reinforcing it is likely more effective than adding rules. This does, however, place a premium on values being genuinely lived: belief systems that are projected but not enacted are unlikely to perform this function.

## Limitations and Future Research

### Limitations

This study is subject to a number of limitations that should be considered when interpreting its findings. The most evident is the sample size. Eight interviews provide a rich but limited basis from which to draw concrete conclusions, and the findings should be understood as offering depth of insight into a small number of accounts rather than a basis for broad generalization. Related to this, the participants were predominantly Swedish and Scandinavian, with only one describing an integration rooted in a markedly different cultural setting. This composition was not by design, but it means that the cultural interpretation central to the discussion rests on a sample drawn largely from one cultural context, and the single contrasting case can only illustrate, not establish the pattern proposed.

The study is also cross-sectional and retrospective. Participants described integrations that had taken place in the past, in some cases several years earlier, and recalled them in a single

interview. This introduces the possibility of recollection bias and means the study captured participants’ interpretations of events after the fact rather than the integration as it unfolded. The reliance on self-reported accounts adds a further limitation, since each integration was described from a single vantage point, usually that of the acquiring side or an external adviser, and no integration was examined from more than one participant’s perspective. The accounts are therefore one-sided, and the perspective of the acquired organization is largely absent.

A further limitation concerns the data itself. The study used semi-structured interviews as its only source of data, without access to company documents or other secondary material, in part because confidentiality agreements prevent the sharing of internal information. The absence of documentary data means the interview accounts could not be triangulated against other sources.

Limitations also arise from the design of the interview instrument. The levers of control are an analytical framework, and participants did not think or speak in its terms, which required their accounts to be interpreted into the framework after the fact. Combined with this, the framing of some questions may not have elicited the most accurate or complete picture of each lever, particularly the boundary system, where responses were often thin. With the benefit of the data gathered, some questions could have been reframed to probe these areas more effectively, and a large body of data would have made such refinement possible.

Finally, two limitations related to the conduct of the research. The analysis was carried out by a single researcher, without a second coder to verify the interpretation of the data, which places greater weight on the transparency of the analytical process described earlier. The researcher was also relatively inexperienced in conducting research interviews, which may have influenced how some interviews were guided. These limitations are acknowledged openly in the interest of presenting the study’s basis honestly. The accounts are, of course, shaped by participants’ own perspectives, though within an interpretivist study this subjectivity is treated as the object of inquiry rather than as a flaw to be eliminated.

### Future Research

The limitations above, together with the findings, point to several avenues for future research. The most direct follows from cultural interpretation at the center of this study. Because that interpretation rests on a predominantly Scandinavian sample, it would benefit from comparative research drawing participants from a wider range of cultural settings, designed specifically to test whether the weakness of boundary systems and the apparent substitution of belief systems for them hold beyond the contexts examined here. A comparative design of this kind would allow the cultural argument to be examined directly rather than inferred from a single contrasting case.

A second avenue concerns the application of the levers of control framework to post-merger integration more broadly. This study has approached that application qualitatively and on a small scale, and there is room to examine the same questions with a larger sample and, potentially, through quantitative methods that could test whether the patterns observed here, particularly the uneven use of the levers and the relationship between belief and boundary systems appear consistently across a larger population of integrations.

A third avenue is methodological. Future studies could seek to incorporate multiple perspectives on the same integration, drawing on participants from both the acquiring and the acquired organizations, which would address the single-perspective limitation of the present study and offer a more complete picture of how the levers are experienced from different positions. Where access can be arranged, the inclusion of secondary or documentary data alongside interviews would strengthen such research, provided it is set up in a way that allows the data to be shared and used, allowing the interview accounts to be triangulated against other evidence.

Taken together, these directions suggest that the contribution of this study is best understood as a starting point. It proposes a way of understanding how managers use the levers of control during integration, and how that use is shaped by context, that future research is well placed to test, refine, and extend.
