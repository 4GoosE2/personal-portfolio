# Summary


This research examines the extent to which EQT, a Swedish private equity firm managing 136 billion EUR in assets, balances People, Planet, and Profit in its sustainability strategy. The study applies the Triple Bottom Line framework and Sustainable Development Goals as analytical lenses to assess whether EQT achieves genuine balance among the three dimensions or prioritizes financial returns over social and environmental commitments.

Using qualitative document analysis, the research examines EQT’s Annual and Sustainability Reports from 2021 and 2024, focusing on SDG 5, Gender Equality, and SDG 13, Climate Action. The analysis distinguishes between measurable indicators and aspirational commitments, comparing stated goals with reported outcomes to identify gaps between sustainability claims and disclosed data.

The findings reveal that EQT does not achieve genuine Triple Bottom Line balance. In the People dimension, a 47 percent gender pay gap contradicts commitments to equal pay, while minimal transparency exists regarding working conditions for the 650 000 employees in portfolio companies. In the Planet dimension, operational emissions achievements address less than 0,05 percent of EQT’s total climate footprint, with financed emissions more than 2 000 times larger receiving proportionally less accountability. Most critically, EQT explicitly positions sustainability as instrumental to financial performance, stating that climate action and diversity initiatives are “important to drive long-term financial performance” rather than intrinsic responsibilities.

The study concludes that EQT exemplifies John Elkington’s critique that companies adopt Triple Bottom Line language without transforming underlying profit priorities, treating sustainability as a value creation tool rather than a fundamental constraint on economic activity.

# 1. Introduction 

## 
1.1 Purpose


Private Equity has become an increasingly influential force in the global economy, with firms managing trillions of dollars in assets and shaping business practices across industries worldwide. As sustainability considerations intensify among investors, regulators, and society, questions arise about whether private equity firms genuinely integrate environmental and social commitments alongside financial objectives, or whether sustainability serves primarily as a tool for value creation and risk management.

Following John Elkington’s 2018 critique that companies have co-opted the Triple Bottom Line framework without transforming underlying profit priorities, examining how major private equity firms approach sustainability becomes particularly relevant. This research therefore aims to assess how EQT, one of Europe’s largest private equity firms managing 136 billion EUR in assets, balances People, Planet, and Profit in its sustainability strategy. The analysis seeks to identify gaps between stated commitments and measurable outcomes, contributing to an understanding of sustainability practices within the private equity industry.


## 1.2 Research Question


To what extent does EQT balance People, Planet, and Profit in its sustainability strategy, and where are the gaps?


# 2. Literature Review


## 2.1 Sustainability


Sustainable development requires a balanced approach addressing environmental concerns alongside economic growth. In 1987, the United Nations’ World Commission on Environment development defined sustainability as “meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs” (World Commission on Environment and Development, 1987). This definition has become the most widely accepted and establishes the core principle that today’s development should not negatively affect future generations’ ability to meet their needs.


## 2.2 The Triple Bottom Line


The Triple Bottom Line is an accounting framework developed by John Elkington in 1994 to measure corporate performance beyond traditional financial metrics. The framework incorporates three dimensions, that are People, Planet, and Profit, to assess a company’s commitment to social responsibility and environmental sustainability over time (Slaper and Hall, 2011).

However, measuring these dimensions presents challenges. While profit is easily quantified in monetary terms, social and environmental impacts are considerably harder to measure (Slaper and Hall, 2011).

Elkington himself has since questioned his framework. In 2018, he argued that companies continue to prioritize profit targets while treating people and planet goals as secondary, noting that “the Triple Bottom Line has failed to bury the single bottom line paradigm” (Elkington, 2018).

### 2.2.1. People


The People dimension includes all stakeholders affected by a company’s operations, including employees, communities, supply chain participants, and future generations (Slaper and Hall, 2011). Companies must respect internationally recognized standards such as UN Global Compact principles and International Labour Organization conventions, covering elimination of forced and child labor, freedom of association, and non-discrimination. Organizations must ensure safe working environments, fair wages, reasonable working hours, and promote inclusive workplaces with zero tolerance for discrimination or harassment (Slaper and Hall, 2011).


### 2.2.2 Planet


The Planet dimension examines how business operations affect natural resources, biodiversity, and  climate stability (Slaper and Hall, 2011). Companies must measure and reduce greenhouse gas emissions across their value chain, manage resources efficiently through circular economy practices, and assess impacts on biodiversity and ecosystems. The dimension also considers climate adaptation, recognizing that companies must build resilience against physical climate risks such as extreme weather events that can disrupt operations and supply chains  (Slaper and Hall, 2011).


### 2.2.3 Profit


The Profit dimension addresses economic sustainability, extending beyond profit maximization to emphasize that businesses must maintain financial health through ethical and responsible practices (Slaper and Hall, 2011). This includes paying fair wages, fulfilling financial responsibilities to creditors and society, and selecting suppliers and partners that align with sustainability values rather than solely on cost. The dimension challenges businesses to balance immediate financial returns with investment, ensuring long-term economic sustainability for both the company and broader society (Slaper and Hall, 2011).


## 2.3 Sustainable Development Goals


The Sustainable Development Goals, adopted by all 193 UN member states in  September 2015, represent a global framework of 17 goals designed to address pressing challenges including poverty, inequality, and environmental protection by 2030. The SDGs are universal, applying equally to all countries, and interconnected, where progress in one goal influences achievement of others. Each goal is divided into specific measurable targets (United Nations, 2015).

Responsibility for achieving the SDGs is distributed across multiple stakeholders. Governments create and enforce policies, the United Nations monitor progress, and businesses are recognized as critical actors with the capacity to develop innovative solutions and direct investments toward SDG-aligned projects (United Nations, 2015).

This research focuses on two SDGs relevant to private equity sustainability strategies. SDG 5, Gender Equality, aims to achieve gender equality and empower all women and girls, including equal representation in leadership and equal pay for work of equal value. SDG 13, Climate Action calls for urgent action to combat climate change through mitigation of greenhouse gas emissions and adaptation to climate-related risks (United Nations, 2015).

## 2.4 Private Equity


The global financial market consists of the public market, where companies sell shares through stock exchanges, and the private market where companies provide professional investors with equity stakes in exchange for funding. Private equity firms invest in companies using capital raised from institutional investors such as pension funds, sovereign wealth funds and insurance companies. These firms acquire ownership, where firms work directly with company management to drive strategic changes, operational improvements, and growth initiatives (Malik and Dhankar, 2017).

Until the 1980s, private equity was predominantly an American phenomenon. During the late 1980s and early 1990s, Nordic countries witnessed the emergence of private equity firms, initially focusing on domestic investments in industrial companies. The industry has evolved from a niche investment strategy into a major force within global finance, with significant influence over corporate operations, employment, and sustainability practices (Malik and Dhankar, 2017).

The relationship between private equity and sustainability has become increasingly significant. Through their investment decisions, private equity firms directly impact sustainability by choosing which sectors and companies receive funding, positioning them as influential actors in addressing sustainability challenges. However, the financial sector also faces scrutiny regarding greenwashing, where companies overstate environmental commitments without corresponding measurable action (Weber and Saravade, 2019).


# 3. Method


This research employs a qualitative case study approach using document analysis to examine EQT’s sustainability strategy. The primary data sources are EQT’s Annual and Sustainability Reports from 2021 and 2024, selected to enable temporal comparison of stated commitments against measurable outcomes. Additional context was gathered from EQT’s corporate website.

The analytical approach maps EQT’s reported initiatives to the Triple Bottom Line dimensions and assesses performance against SDG 5, Gender Equality, and SDG 13, Climate Action. The analysis distinguishes between measurable indicators and aspirational commitments, comparing stated goals with reported outcomes to identify gaps or contradictions.

This research has two primary limitations. First, it relies on self-reported corporate data, which may reflect selective disclosure practices. Second, limited access to internal metrics, particularly regarding portfolio company operations, constrains the depth of analysis possible regarding EQT’s broader value chain impacts.


# 4. Results


## 4.1 EQT


EQT is a global private equity firm founded the year 1994 in Stockholm, with roots in the Wallenberg family through Investor AB. Over three decades, EQT has transformed from a Nordic-focused firm into a global investment leader operating in more than 25 countries (EQT, 2024).

EQT operates as a purpose-driven investment organization focused on active ownership strategies. The firm raises capital from institutional investors such as pension funds and sovereign wealth funds, then acquires ownership stakes in companies and real assets. EQT's approach emphasizes active ownership, meaning the firm works closely with the management of its portfolio companies (the businesses in which EQT invests) to drive operational improvements, strategic changes, and value creation over typical investment periods of three to seven years (EQT, 2024).
As of 2024, EQT manages approximately 136 billion EUR across more than 50 active funds, with portfolio companies employing around 650 000 people globally. Since becoming publicly listed in 2019, EQT has expanded from 37 billion EUR to 136 billion EUR in assets under management (EQT, 2024). EQT positions sustainability as both a value creation opportunity and a means to generate positive impact for all stakeholders (EQT, 2024).


### 4.2 Value Chain and Sustainable Development Goals


EQT's value chain extends beyond its own operations to encompass portfolio companies, the businesses in which EQT funds invest and typically hold controlling stakes. These portfolio companies collectively employ approximately 650 000 people globally (EQT, 2024, p. 6), representing a significantly larger sustainability impact than EQT AB Group's approximately 3 000 employees.
Within EQT's own operations, women represented 25 percent of Investment Advisory Professionals in 2021 (EQT, 2021, p. 82), increasing marginally to 29 percent by 2024 (EQT, 2024, p. 165). However, the 2024 report reveals a 47 percent gender pay gap (EQT, 2024, p. 146), persisting from 2021 when men earned twice women's average compensation in non-investment roles (EQT, 2021, p. 83).


### 4.2.1 SDG 5: Gender Equality


For portfolio companies, EQT established a target in 2021 for 40 percent women as independent board members, reaching 35 percent by 2024 (EQT, 2024, p. 165). Yet the report notes "these ambitions are not formal targets" (EQT, 2024, p. 144), undermining accountability. EQT provides no data on gender representation or pay equity within portfolio companies' broader workforces beyond board composition.
This selective reporting, tracking governance-level metrics while omitting operational workforce conditions, suggests EQT prioritizes metrics visible to investors over comprehensive accountability for the 650 000 workers whose conditions represent the firm's largest social impact.


### 4.2.2 SDG 13: Climate Action


EQT's operational emissions total approximately 23 000 tonnes CO2 equivalent annually, while financed emissions through portfolio investments reach approximately 48 million tonnes, more than   2 000 times larger (EQT, 2024, p. 134). EQT acknowledges "the underlying investments in EQT funds have a significantly larger impact in absolute terms than EQT AB Group" (EQT, 2024, p. 126), yet detailed climate reporting focuses predominantly on operational achievements.
Within the value chain, 52 portfolio companies representing 65 percent of invested equity have validated science-based targets (EQT, 2024, p. 132). However, the 2024 report notes "uncertainties regarding the probability of climate scenarios" (EQT, 2024, p. 128), indicating incomplete visibility into portfolio company emissions.


## 4.3 Triple Bottom Line


### 4.3.1People 


EQT commits to international human rights standards and zero tolerance for discrimination, yet its treatment of people reveals significant gaps between these stated commitments and measurable outcomes.
EQT's Code of Ethics commits to "equal pay for work of equal value" and "zero tolerance towards any form of discrimination, harassment, or bullying" (EQT, 2024, p. 140). However, the 47 percent gender pay gap documented in 2024 (EQT, 2024, p. 146) directly contradicts these principles, with men earning nearly twice what women earn on average. This inequality persists despite EQT's claims of using "thorough and objective benchmarks" for compensation (EQT, 2024, p. 143).
The framing of diversity initiatives reveals instrumental treatment of people. Rather than positioning diversity as a fundamental human right, EQT frames it as a business imperative, noting "if inclusion efforts are not properly implemented, there is a risk that EQT might be losing talent" (EQT, 2024, p. 144). This profit-oriented justification aligns with Elkington's critique that companies prioritize business cases over intrinsic commitments to people.
The approximately 650 000 employees in portfolio companies represent EQT's largest sphere of human impact, yet the firm provides minimal transparency regarding their conditions. EQT acknowledges "higher exposures of working condition violations such as within health and safety" in some sectors (EQT, 2024, p. 148), but immediately minimizes this by stating "the negative impacts are neither widespread or systemic" (EQT, 2024, p. 148) without providing supporting data.
Despite policy commitments to "decent working conditions, including minimum wages, working hours, health and safety" (EQT, 2024, p. 149), EQT's sustainability report provides no aggregated metrics on wage levels, working hours, or injury rates across portfolio companies. This pattern of aspirational policies without measurable accountability reflects Elkington's observation that companies adopt TBL language without transforming underlying priorities.



### 4.3.2 Planet


EQT's approach to the Planet dimension demonstrates a significant misalignment between operational achievements and value chain responsibility. The firm frames climate action explicitly as instrumental to financial performance, stating "climate resilience and decarbonization are components EQT considers important to drive long-term financial performance" (EQT, 2024, p. 127), positioning environmental protection as a means to profit rather than an intrinsic commitment.
Within its own operations, EQT has achieved demonstrable environmental commitments. The firm sources 100 percent renewable electricity for its offices, reducing Scope 2 market-based emissions to near zero (EQT, 2024, p. 133). Total operational emissions from business travel and office energy consumption total approximately 23 000 tonnes CO2 equivalent annually, with science-based reduction targets validated by the Science Based Targets initiative (EQT, 2024, p. 132).
However, these operational achievements address less than 0,05 percent of EQT's total climate footprint. Despite this acknowledgment, EQT's 2024 sustainability report dedicates approximately 15 pages to operational climate initiatives while providing only 2 pages of aggregated portfolio company emissions data, inverting the proportion of actual environmental impact. The firm explicitly acknowledges that "the underlying investments in EQT funds have a significantly larger impact in absolute terms than EQT AB Group" (EQT, 2024, p. 126), yet detailed climate reporting and concrete action plans focus predominantly on operational improvements.
EQT's financed emissions through portfolio company investments total approximately 48,4 million tonnes CO2 equivalent, more than 2 000 times larger than operational emissions (EQT, 2024, p. 134). While 52 portfolio companies have validated science-based targets (EQT, 2024, p. 132), the sustainability report notes "uncertainties regarding the probability of climate scenarios" and acknowledges that emissions data relies partially on estimates with only 86 percent coverage from actual portfolio company data (EQT, 2024, pp. 128, 135).
This pattern reflects Elkington's critique that companies measure and manage what serves profit objectives while minimizing attention to larger environmental impacts.



### 4.3.3 Profit


The Profit dimension within the Triple Bottom Line framework examines whether companies generate economic value while balancing social and environmental responsibilities (Slaper and Hall, 2011). EQT's approach reveals that sustainability is positioned explicitly as a means to profit rather than a parallel or constraining objective.
Throughout EQT's reporting, sustainability is consistently framed as instrumental to financial performance. The firm states that "climate resilience and decarbonization are important components EQT considers important to drive long-term financial performance" (EQT, 2024, p. 127), positioning environmental action as a driver of returns. Similarly, diversity initiatives are justified through "financial benefit from strengthened inclusion efforts leading to high performing teams, innovation and better decision-making" (EQT, 2024, p. 125), rather than as commitments to social justice.
This framing extends to portfolio companies, where EQT aims to "support portfolio companies to grow sustainability-themed revenue streams" and "design exit processes to highlight the sustainability-themed products and services...in order to capture investor interest in the sustainability megatrend and higher company valuation" (EQT, 2024, p. 165). Sustainability becomes a source of competitive advantage and premium valuations rather than an intrinsic responsibility.
Despite emphasizing sustainability as a value creation opportunity, EQT provides minimal transparency on actual financial outcomes. The 2024 report acknowledges that "work is ongoing to define and measure sustainability-themed revenue streams" (EQT, 2024, p. 165) without disclosing what percentage of portfolio revenue derives from sustainable products or services. While 32 percent of portfolio companies have sustainability-linked incentives (EQT, 2024, p. 125), 68 percent lack direct financial mechanisms connecting compensation to sustainability performance.
This pattern exemplifies Elkington's critique that companies prioritize profit targets while treating people and planet dimensions as subordinate, inverting the TBL principle that economic activity should operate within social and environmental boundaries.

# 5. Conclusion


Research Question: To what extent does EQT balance People, Planet, and Profit in its sustainability strategy, and where are the gaps?
This research examined the extent to which EQT balances People, Planet, and Profit in its sustainability strategy through the Triple Bottom Line framework. The analysis reveals that EQT does not achieve genuine balance among the three dimensions, instead prioritizing profit while treating people and planet as instrumental to financial performance rather than intrinsic commitments. 

Regarding the People dimension, significant gaps persist between EQT’s stated commitments and measurable outcomes. A 47 percent gender pay gap contradicts the claim of equal pay, while diversity initiatives are framed as talent retention strategies rather than human rights imperatives. For the 650 000 employees in portfolio companies, EQT provides minimal transparency on working conditions, wage levels, or injury rates, acknowledging working condition violations while simultaneously minimizing their significance as “neither widespread or systemic” without supporting data.

The Planet dimension demonstrates a significant misalignment between operational achievements and value chain responsibility. While EQT has achieved measurable climate progress in its own operations, these efforts address less than 0,05 percent of the firm’s total climate footprint. Financed emissions through portfolio investments are more than 2 000 times larger than operational emissions, yet receive proportionally less detailed reporting and accountability.

Most critically, EQT explicitly positions sustainability as a tool for value creation and financial performance rather than a parallel objective. Throughout its reporting, environmental and social commitments are justified through their contribution to returns, investor interest, and competitive advantage. This profit-first framing reflects Elkington’s observation that companies adopt Triple Bottom Line language without transforming underlying priorities, treating sustainability as instrumental to economic success rather than as a fundamental constraint on profit-seeking behavior.

# 
6. Recommendations


To achieve genuine Triple Bottom Line balance, EQT must address the fundamental gaps between aspirational commitments and measurable accountability identified in this analysis.
EQT should establish binding targets for gender equality rather than non-binding "ambitions," including specific timelines to close the 47 percent gender pay gap. For portfolio companies, EQT must expand transparency beyond board composition to include aggregated workforce data on wage levels, working hours, and occupational injury rates across the 650 000 employees in its value chain, published annually with year-over-year comparisons.
EQT should align the depth and detail of its financed emissions reporting with its operational emissions reporting, given that portfolio company emissions represent over 99,95 percent of its total climate footprint. This includes publishing portfolio-level data on renewable energy adoption, emissions intensity by sector, and progress toward decarbonization targets.
Most fundamentally, EQT must move beyond framing sustainability exclusively as a value creation tool. Genuine TBL balance requires treating people and planet as stakeholders with intrinsic rights rather than instrumental inputs to profit. This shift requires establishing sustainability commitments that apply regardless of their contribution to financial returns, acknowledging that authentic sustainability sometimes constrains rather than enhances profit-seeking behavior.
